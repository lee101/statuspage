# Outreach Research

Use this as a guide for helpful public replies, not for scraping email addresses or sending unsolicited bulk messages.

## Help-first rule

Only mention an Applied AI NZ product when it directly answers the thread. Lead with the useful technical answer, then add one short disclosure such as: "I build statuspage.app.nz / Applied AI NZ tools, so I am biased, but here is the practical checklist I would use."

## Relevant thread types found

- SaaS founders asking whether public status pages matter for early-stage products.
- Side-project and micro-SaaS builders comparing uptime monitoring, incident communication, custom domains, and pricing.
- Developers discussing model-routing quality, provider latency, OpenRouter-style routing, and fallback strategy.
- New Zealand workers and business owners discussing practical AI adoption at work.
- No-code and AI builder threads where people are trying to turn quick prototypes into maintainable products.

## Useful reply angles

- Status page threads: share a short checklist for monitors, external hosting, custom domain, SSL, subscriber expectations, and incident wording. Mention statuspage.app.nz only if someone asks for a simple hosted option.
- AI API/model routing threads: discuss latency, provider quality, fallbacks, cost controls, and monitoring model providers separately. Mention Text-Generator.io or Open Paths only as examples.
- AI adoption in NZ threads: explain where applied AI is already useful: document processing, support workflows, internal tools, voice dictation, reporting, and prototype-to-production engineering.
- Builder/prototype threads: offer Web Fiddle, Site Sim, Netwrck, AI Art Generator, or SimplexGen only when the thread is about browser prototypes, generated media, website simulation, or 3D asset workflows.
- Growth threads: mention Experiment Flow or Evangeler only when the thread is specifically about A/B testing, personalization, affiliates, partner programs, or payout workflows.

## Example reply shape

```text
The practical split I would use is:

1. One external check for the public app.
2. One check for the API or checkout path customers actually depend on.
3. One separate status URL that is not hosted on the same failing app.
4. A tiny incident template: what is affected, what still works, next update time.

Disclosure: I build statuspage.app.nz, so I am biased, but the checklist matters more than the vendor. A simple hosted page is enough for most early SaaS teams.
```
